## Governance

- **Governance:** The way an organization directs, controls, measures, and improves security.
- Governance defines who makes security decisions, who owns risk, and how security performance is reported.
- Good governance helps align security with business goals.

Governance answers questions such as:
- Who owns security risk?
- Who approves policies?
- Who can accept risk?
- How are controls measured?
- How are exceptions handled?

## Compliance

- **Compliance:** Following laws, regulations, contracts, standards, and internal policies.
- Compliance helps prove that required controls and processes are being followed.
- Compliance does not automatically mean an organization is fully secure, but it creates accountability.

Examples of compliance areas:
- Privacy requirements
- Payment card data protection
- Healthcare data protection
- Financial data protection
- Security awareness training
- Access review requirements
- Incident reporting requirements

## Policies, Standards, Procedures, and Guidelines

- **Policy:** High-level management direction.
  - Example: All users must protect company data.

- **Standard:** Specific mandatory requirement.
  - Example: Passwords must be at least 14 characters.

- **Procedure:** Step-by-step instruction.
  - Example: How to disable a user account during offboarding.

- **Guideline:** Recommended best practice.
  - Example: Use a password manager for unique passwords.

- **Baseline:** Minimum approved configuration.
  - Example: Windows workstation hardening baseline.

## Security Policy Examples

Common security policies include:
- Acceptable use policy
- Password policy
- Access control policy
- Data classification policy
- Remote access policy
- Incident response policy
- Change management policy
- Backup policy
- Vendor risk policy
- Mobile device policy

## Control Ownership

- **Control Owner:** Person or team responsible for a security control.
- Controls need owners so someone is accountable for implementation, review, evidence, and improvement.

Example:
- HR may own onboarding and offboarding steps.
- IT may own endpoint patching.
- Security may own SIEM alert rules.
- Management may own risk acceptance.

## Compliance Evidence

Evidence proves that controls are operating.

Examples:
- Policies and procedures
- Access review exports
- Screenshots of secure configurations
- Audit logs
- Vulnerability scan reports
- Patch reports
- Backup test results
- Security awareness completion records
- Incident response records

## Frameworks and Regulations

Common frameworks and regulations may include:
- NIST Cybersecurity Framework
- ISO 27001
- SOC 2
- PCI DSS
- HIPAA
- GDPR
- CIS Controls

## Key Takeaways

- Governance defines how security is managed.
- Compliance proves required controls and processes are followed.
- Policies explain what must happen; procedures explain how to do it.
